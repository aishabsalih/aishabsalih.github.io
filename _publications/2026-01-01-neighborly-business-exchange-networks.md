This paper studies how informal labor relationships shape the allocation of wage work as rural labor markets develop. In many village economies, reciprocal labor exchange and hired labor coexist, raising a central question: when agricultural labor demand rises, do households substitute away from informal obligations toward anonymous spot-market hiring, or do existing relationships mediate the transition to wage contracts? We answer this question using the Townsend Thai monthly panel, which links household labor outcomes to dyadic transaction records that identify whether hired workers were prior exchange partners. A monthly Bartik shifter, constructed from predetermined village crop specialization and national producer-price growth, provides short-run variation in local farm-labor demand. Average labor quantities respond weakly, consistent with common village-level shocks in relatively closed labor markets. The main response is instead compositional: hired-labor transactions are more likely to occur within sampled pre-existing labor relationships, especially baseline dual ties, than with sampled new partners. The evidence suggests that declining reciprocal exchange need not imply the disappearance of village labor networks; rather, persistent ties can serve as relational infrastructure for wage transactions.

*With Eva Raiber (Aix-Marseille School of Economics, Aix-Marseille University, CNRS)*

We examine how climate shocks affect religious contributions in Nigeria 
using household panel data from the Nigeria General Household Survey 
(2010--2019) combined with the Standardised 
Precipitation--Evapotranspiration Index (SPEI). Severe drought and 
pluvial shocks have opposite effects on religious giving. Two severe 
drought months increase religious expenditure and raise the probability 
of any contribution by 9 to 10 percentage points. Merging LSMS 
consumption aggregates, we show that this increase occurs even as 
total household consumption falls by 16 percent, constituting direct 
evidence of deliberate reallocation toward religion rather than a 
mechanical income effect. Severe pluvial shocks reduce religious 
giving and lower the giving probability by 5 to 7 percentage points. 
The drought effect is concentrated among Christian households, in 
urban areas, and among higher-consumption households, and is not 
confined to crop-holding households, pointing toward a community 
engagement channel in which religious organisations mobilise giving 
in response to a salient and widespread shock. Afrobarometer evidence 
corroborates this interpretation: perceived drought severity is 
associated with greater reliance on religious coping networks and 
higher trust in religious leaders, while objective pluvial exposure 
reduces that trust. The pluvial effect is negative and uniform across 
sectors and religious groups, consistent with a resource constraint 
channel.

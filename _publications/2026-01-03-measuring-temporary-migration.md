*With Timothée Demont (Aix-Marseille School of Economics, Aix-Marseille University, CNRS)*

Temporary and seasonal migration is an important adjustment margin for rural households in developing economies, especially where agricultural income is seasonal and formal insurance is limited. Yet empirical estimates of temporary migration vary widely across surveys. This paper treats survey design as a primary source of this variation and develops a framework for interpreting what different instruments measure. We distinguish three population estimands: point prevalence, person-time prevalence, and annual incidence, and map common household-survey designs to the quantities they identify.
Using the Tanzania National Panel Survey, we show that a snapshot roster indicator misses 46–63 percent of the absence recorded by a twelve-month recall indicator on the same individuals. Cross-country LSMS-ISA comparisons show that the same nested logic holds where roster items are comparable, although levels depend on item wording. Because recall is incomplete and roster dropouts remain unobserved, these estimates are lower bounds on snapshot undercount among listed members rather than estimates of true prevalence. The paper then shows how the framework organizes existing evidence and motivates a low-cost elicitation module that records spell timing, purpose, return expectations, and destination outcomes.
